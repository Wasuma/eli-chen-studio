Notion for Occupational Therapists: Client Tracking, Treatment Plans, and Functional Outcomes

Published March 2026 | 10 minute read

Occupational therapists manage complex caseloads where each client has unique functional goals: returning to work after injury, managing chronic pain during daily activities, achieving independence in ADLs (Activities of Daily Living), or developing life skills. You're documenting functional assessments, tracking progress toward ADL goals, managing referrals, and justifying medical necessity to insurance.

Most OTs use paper charts, scattered digital notes, and email follow-ups. The result? You're spending time searching for client information instead of planning interventions, and it's harder to demonstrate client progress to insurance companies and physicians.

Notion can be your complete occupational therapy workspace—centralizing client records, functional goal tracking, intervention documentation, and outcome measurement in one system.

Why Notion for Occupational Therapists

  1. Functional goal tracking — Document ADL/IADL goals and measure progress systematically
  2. Intervention documentation — Track which occupations/activities were used in each session
  3. Outcome measurement — Standardized assessments (COPM, FSIQ, etc.) tracked over time
  4. Insurance justification — Generate reports showing medical necessity and progress
  5. Privacy — Unlike cloud-based EMRs, you control your data completely
  6. Customizable templates — Build documentation matching your specific populations (pediatric, geriatric, hand therapy, etc.)

What You Can Track in Notion

Client Profiles

  • Demographics: age, diagnosis, onset date, living situation, occupation/role
  • Referral information: referring provider, insurance, authorization hours
  • Occupational profile: roles (worker, parent, student), routines, meaningful activities
  • Performance history: previous injuries, risk factors, current limitations
  • Environmental factors: home setup, work accommodations, accessibility

Functional Assessments

  • Initial assessment: functional performance, occupational history, goals
  • Standardized assessments: COPM (Canadian Occupational Performance Measure), FMA (Fugl-Meyer), FSIQ (Functional Status Index)
  • ADL/IADL assessment: independence levels in self-care, instrumental activities, work/leisure
  • Home/work assessment: environmental barriers and supports
  • Track assessment dates for re-testing at regular intervals

Treatment Goals

  • Functional goals: specific, measurable, occupational (e.g., "return to managing medication schedule independently")
  • Goal timeline: short-term (1-4 weeks) and long-term (8+ weeks)
  • Progress measurement: baseline performance, target performance, current status
  • Linked interventions: which activities address which goals

Session Documentation

  • Session template: date, duration, occupations/activities used, client performance
  • Functional progress: performance change, barriers encountered, adaptations made
  • Intervention specifics: what occupation was used, how it addresses goals, client response
  • Plan for next session: skills to practice, home programs, environmental modifications
  • Hours tracked: billable hours, insurance authorization tracking

Home Program Tracking

  • Home exercise/activity program assigned to each client
  • Frequency and duration recommendations
  • Compliance tracking: is client doing the program?
  • Modifications: what's working, what needs adjustment?

Outcome Tracking

  • Functional independence: ADL/IADL performance over time
  • Pain/fatigue: if relevant to goals, track changes
  • Return to occupation: progress toward work, hobby, or role resumption
  • Quality of life: client-reported satisfaction with progress

Core Databases for Occupational Therapists

Essential Tables

  • Clients — Demographics, diagnosis, referral, insurance, status
  • Assessments — Assessment type, date, scores, findings
  • Goals — Functional goal, baseline, target, deadline, status
  • Sessions — Date, occupations used, performance, progress notes
  • Home Programs — Activity, frequency, compliance tracking
  • Outcomes — Goal completion, ADL/IADL gains, client satisfaction

How to Set Up Your OT Practice System

  1. Create core databases: Clients, Assessments, Goals, Sessions, Home Programs
  2. Build intake template matching your assessment protocol
  3. Create session note template with occupation-centered focus:
    • Which meaningful occupation/activity was used?
    • How did client perform?
    • What did they learn or improve?
    • What's the home program for this week?
  4. Set up views for your workflow:
    • Active caseload: all current clients, next session date
    • Authorization tracking: billable hours remaining, approaching limits
    • Goal progress: % toward functional goal completion
    • Assessment schedule: when are reassessments due?
  5. Build formulas:
    • Hours used ÷ hours authorized
    • Sessions to goal completion (at current progress rate)
    • ADL independence gains (baseline vs. current vs. target)

Real Example: Hand Therapy Practice

Jennifer, a certified hand therapist with a private practice, manages 15-20 active clients post-surgery and injury. Using Notion she:

  • Tracks functional hand use goals: grip strength, ROM, fine motor return
  • Documents each session: exercises performed, functional improvements, pain levels
  • Assigns home programs: finger exercises, scar management, ADL training
  • Measures outcomes: tracks ROM, strength, and functional hand use month-over-month
  • Generates insurance reports: shows client progress to justify continued treatment

Before Notion, Jennifer spent time searching through paper charts to find baseline measurements or justify medical necessity to insurance. Now she generates progress reports in minutes, showing objective improvement in ROM and functional hand use.

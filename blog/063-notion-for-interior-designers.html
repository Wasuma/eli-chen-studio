The Designer's Project Management Challenge

Managing a design project means orchestrating dozens of decisions across multiple vendors and stakeholders:

  • Inspiration and mood boards scattered across Pinterest, Houzz, and email boards
  • Fabric swatches, paint colors, and material samples stored in folders and labeled envelopes
  • Furniture specifications and dimensions in scattered Word documents and Canva files
  • Client preferences and feedback across Slack, email, and voice calls
  • Vendor quotes for flooring, paint, furniture, lighting — each in a separate email or spreadsheet
  • Project budget tracked in one spreadsheet, actual spending in another
  • Timeline and approvals managed in your head or in a generic project tool
  • Floor plan sketches and specification sheets across multiple files

The result: design decisions take twice as long because you're constantly hunting for references, rechecking material names, and asking clients the same questions twice.

How to Structure Notion for Design Projects

Core Databases

1. Projects & Clients — Master project hub.

  • Project name, client name, location, project address
  • Scope (single room, full renovation, new construction, commercial)
  • Budget, status, timeline (kick-off date, deadline)
  • Design style direction (modern, traditional, transitional, eclectic)
  • Views: "Active projects," "Budget status," "By designer," "Overdue approvals"

2. Rooms & Spaces — Project breakdown.

  • Room name, dimensions, natural light, existing fixtures
  • Design direction for the space, assigned designer
  • Linked to project and floor plan
  • Design decisions made for that room (color palette, materials, finishes)

3. Design Mood Board & References — Inspiration gallery.

  • Upload or link inspiration images
  • Tag by project, room, design element (color, texture, style)
  • Store links to Houzz, Pinterest, designer websites
  • Notes on why you love each reference (color palette, proportions, material texture)

4. Furniture & Furnishings — Product catalog.

  • Product name, category (sofa, dining table, chairs), brand/vendor
  • Dimensions, available finishes, upholstery options
  • Cost, lead time, special order requirements
  • Product images, product links, sales contact
  • Views: "Sofas," "Dining tables," "By vendor," "Quick reference cards"

5. Materials & Finishes — Specification tracker.

  • Material type (flooring, paint, tile, wallpaper, hardware, trim)
  • Product name, color, finish, supplier
  • Dimensions/specifications, cost per unit, coverage area
  • Sample status (ordered, received, approved, rejected)
  • Linked to rooms and projects

6. Vendor & Supplier Directory — Vendor hub.

  • Vendor name, category (flooring, paint, furniture, contractor)
  • Contact person, phone, email, website
  • Terms, delivery fees, return policies
  • Notes on reliability, quality, lead times, and your recommendation rating
  • Track quotes and pricing history

7. Project Timeline & Approvals — Schedule & spec tracking.

  • Design phase tasks: inspiration gathering, concept design, mood board, palette selection
  • Development phase: specification, material selection, vendor selection
  • Procurement phase: ordering, payment, tracking deliveries
  • Installation phase: installation schedule, contractor coordination
  • Status for each phase: pending, in progress, complete, approved

8. Budget & Costs — Financial tracking.

  • Budget allocation by category: furniture, flooring, paint, fixtures, labor, contingency
  • Actual quotes and spending by vendor
  • Dashboard showing total budget vs. actual by category
  • Alerts when over budget in any category

9. Procurement & Orders — Order tracking.

  • What's ordered, from whom, order date, cost
  • Expected delivery date, actual delivery date
  • Payment status, warranty information
  • Quick view of pending deliveries and overdue orders

Design Project Workflow in Notion

Phase 1: Discovery & Concept (Weeks 1-2)

  1. Create project in Projects database with client info and budget
  2. Create Rooms & Spaces database entries for each space in the project
  3. Gather inspiration: curate mood board, capture color palettes, style direction
  4. Share mood board with client via portal for feedback
  5. Document client preferences and final direction approved

Phase 2: Design Development (Weeks 3-6)

  1. For each room, develop material and finish specifications
  2. Research and add furniture options to your Product database
  3. Get quotes from vendors for major items (flooring, paint, furniture)
  4. Update budget spreadsheet with actual quotes
  5. Create color palette and finish schedule (which paint color, which tile, which hardware finish)
  6. Share specification document with client for approval

Phase 3: Procurement (Weeks 7-10)

  1. Client approves all specifications
  2. Place orders and log in Procurement database
  3. Track delivery dates and progress
  4. Coordinate with contractors on installation timing

Phase 4: Installation & Completion (Week 11-12)

  1. Coordinate deliveries and installation
  2. Track items as they arrive and are installed
  3. Document final project photos
  4. Finalize budget reconciliation
  5. Archive project (or save as template for next similar project)

Common Questions

Can I use this for architecture projects with CAD files?

Yes. Store links to your Revit files, CAD drawings, 3D renderings in the project. Notion becomes your project management hub while CAD stays your design tool.

How do I share this with my clients?

Create a "Client Portal" page or database view with read-only access. Share the link via email. Clients see mood board, timeline, budget overview, and approval checkpoints.

Can I import images into Notion?

Yes! Upload images directly or link to files in OneDrive, Google Drive, or Dropbox. Create a visual gallery of mood boards and reference images.

What if my client changes their mind mid-project?

Log the change request in the project timeline. Update specifications, recalculate budget impact, and track the revised approval. Everything is documented and traceable.
